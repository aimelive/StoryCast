Voices from the Valley: Stories of the Rift

Over eighteen months, producer Amara Diallo traveled along East Africa's Great Rift Valley collecting testimonies from communities whose daily lives straddle geology, tradition, and an accelerating climate.

Story

The Great Rift Valley is one of the world's great geological scars — a fracture in the earth's crust that runs 6,000 kilometers from the Afar Triangle in the north to Mozambique in the south. But to the people who live along its edges, it isn't a scar. It's home.

"The ground moves here. We feel it, sometimes. A trembling — small, like a sleeping animal. My grandmother said it was the earth breathing." — Wanjiru Kamau, Naivasha, Kenya

Amara Diallo spent eighteen months recording conversations across five countries: Ethiopia, Kenya, Tanzania, Malawi, and Mozambique. The result is a mosaic — forty voices, dozens of languages, a thousand perspectives on what it means to live in a place the planet is slowly pulling apart.

On the road and in the recording

Diallo used a portable field recorder and a single directional microphone. "I didn't want the equipment to be the story," she says. "I wanted people to forget it was there." The approach yields something rare in audio documentary: intimacy without performance.

The documentary is organized not chronologically but geographically — moving from north to south along the valley floor, each section anchored by a different landscape, a different climate, a different relationship with water. Water is the through-line. The Rift Valley holds several of Africa's largest freshwater lakes, and their fate — rising in some places, retreating in others — shapes everything.
