About this talk

We tend to think of memory as something stored — tucked away in the brain like a document in a drawer. Jerome Rekart challenges that picture entirely. Drawing on decades of neuroscience research, he argues that memory is better understood as a process of active construction: less like retrieval, more like drawing a map.

"Every time you remember something, you are not playing back a recording. You are making something — and that act of making changes what you will remember next time." — Jerome Rekart

The talk moves between cognitive science and the history of cartography, finding surprising common ground: both maps and memories simplify, emphasise certain features, and inevitably reflect the perspective of whoever made them. Neither is neutral. Neither is fixed.

Why this matters

Rekart's argument has practical implications for how we learn, how we design education, and how we think about reliability in witness testimony. If memory is construction rather than replay, then the conditions under which we remember something matter as much as the original experience.
