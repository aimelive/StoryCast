The Cartographers of Memory

Jerome Rekart makes a compelling case for rethinking memory — not as a filing cabinet, but as a living map that we constantly redraw. A talk about neuroscience, cartography, and what they have in common.

Jerome Rekart

About this talk

Jerome Rekart opens with a crossword clue — "forms in an instant, and lasts a lifetime" — and uses it to dismantle one of the most common misconceptions about memory: that it works like a recording. As a learning scientist trained in the molecular and cellular bases of memory, Rekart knows the reality is far more interesting.

"Learning and memory are actually not really distinct. They're two parts of the same process. You learn something with the sole purpose of holding onto it." — Jerome Rekart

The talk moves from rats navigating the Morris Water Maze, to London cab drivers with measurably larger hippocampi, to the ancient Greek technique of the method of loci — and finds a single thread connecting all of them: the hippocampus, and its role as the brain's map-maker for everything we learn.

What this means for how we learn

Rekart argues that because memory is construction rather than retrieval, the conditions under which we remember something matter as much as the original experience. His practical takeaway — placing new information along a mental route you already know, even something as simple as your kitchen drawers — has been shown in rigorous studies to help medical students learn thousands of anatomical structures.
