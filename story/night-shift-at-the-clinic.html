The Night Shift at the Clinic

The Night Shift in A&E: The NHS at 70. Emergency medicine doctor and filmmaker Dr Salia Ahsan turns the camera on her own department at Ysbyty Gwynedd in Bangor, Wales — following registrar Dr Arvid Schigt through a night-long battle against a waiting room that never empties.

About this documentary

At Ysbyty Gwynedd in Bangor, Wales, nearly 50,000 patients visit A&E every year. In April, almost a third of them waited longer than the four-hour NHS target. Emergency medicine doctor and filmmaker Dr Salia Ahsan decided to find out what a night shift actually looks like from the inside.

"It's extremely busy tonight. There's lots of people in the waiting room who really should be in the department on trolleys, but unfortunately, we have nowhere to put them." — Dr Arvid Schigt

The film follows registrar Dr Arvid Schigt from the 10pm handover — when there's already a seven-hour wait — through to 7am when the day team arrives. Along the way: a seizure patient with no line, a family with a baby who leave after three hours, CT scans approved by radiologists calling in from Australia, and a 23-year-old student brought in by ambulance.

The bigger picture

The documentary was filmed to mark the NHS at 70. Dr Schigt, originally from the Netherlands, is leaving Bangor for a new post with the Flying Doctors in Australia — and like many NHS staff at the time, he is uncertain whether Brexit will allow him to return.
