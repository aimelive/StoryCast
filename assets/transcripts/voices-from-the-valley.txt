[00:00] [Ambient sound: wind, distant cattle, the low tremor of a landscape]

[00:14] AMARA DIALLO (narration): The Great Rift Valley does not announce itself. 
It simply opens in front of you — a drop in the earth's crust so wide and so sudden 
that for a moment, you aren't sure what you're seeing. One step: flat savanna, 
acacia trees, red dust roads. Next step: the world falls away.

[00:38] [Ambient sound fades; footsteps on dry ground]

[00:45] AMARA DIALLO: I arrived in Naivasha in the dry season, when the lake had 
retreated three meters from where it stood in my grandmother's photographs. I had 
come to speak with Wanjiru Kamau, who has fished this lake for forty years.

[01:10] WANJIRU KAMAU (translated from Kikuyu): The ground moves here. We feel it, 
sometimes. A trembling — small, like a sleeping animal. My grandmother said it was 
the earth breathing. I said: good. Let it breathe. It means we are not dead.

[01:42] AMARA DIALLO: Wanjiru has watched the lake level change with each decade. 
She tracks it not by measurement, but by a stand of fever trees near the shore that 
she planted with her father when she was seven years old.

[02:08] WANJIRU KAMAU: When the trees are in the water, there is too much rain coming. 
When the roots are dry and cracked, there is not enough. The trees are my calendar. 
The trees are my science.

[02:35] [Music: a single kora string, sustained]

[02:44] AMARA DIALLO: From Naivasha, I traveled south, into Tanzania, following the 
valley floor through the Serengeti's eastern edge. Here, the rift is less a valley 
than a sky — so wide you forget the walls.

[03:15] [Sound: rain beginning on a metal roof; laughter of children inside]

[03:28] ELIUD MOTAI (Maasai elder, translated): You ask me if I am worried about 
the climate. I say: I have been worried about the rain since I was born. This is 
not new. What is new is — the rain does not answer when you call it. Before, 
you could read the sky. The cattle knew. The birds knew. Now even the birds are 
confused. They arrive early. They leave early. They forget when they are.

[04:20] AMARA DIALLO: Eliud is in his late seventies. He has twelve grandchildren 
and has lived through three periods of severe drought. He is not, he is careful to 
say, afraid.

[04:38] ELIUD MOTAI: Fear is for people who have nowhere to go. We are Maasai. 
We have always moved. The question now is — where do you move when everywhere 
is changing?
